Medical device supply chains are incredibly complex. When manufacturers, suppliers, and sellers operate in informational silos, a small shift in hospital demand can trigger much larger and more costly fluctuations upstream. This pattern is known as the bullwhip effect, and it is one of the main ideas behind this project.

To explore the problem, I built the MedTech Supply Chain Control Tower. The goal was to connect logistical data with financial strategy in a form that is easier to understand, explain, and use. Rather than presenting the project only as a dashboard, I paired an interactive story with a live R Shiny application so that users could first understand the problem and then explore the data themselves.

The project has two parts. The first is an interactive scrollytelling story, available at [this link](https://zw-paulwang.github.io/supply-chain-story/). It walks through the business problem, explains the supply chain structure, and shows why information silos can damage profit margins. The second is the live Control Tower dashboard, available at [this link](https://paul-wang.shinyapps.io/final_project/). The dashboard can be explored with the username `MedTech Worker` and the password `datavisualization`.

The scrollytelling portion is meant to provide context before asking users to interpret a more complex analytical interface. As users scroll, the story introduces the flow of medical devices through the supply chain and gradually builds intuition around where bottlenecks can appear. This was important because data visualization is most useful when the viewer understands the question being asked.

The R Shiny dashboard is where the deeper analysis takes place. It allows users to examine metrics, trends, and operational patterns interactively. One feature is a Sankey diagram that can switch between unit volume and capital mode. In unit volume mode, the diagram shows the movement of inventory through the system. In capital mode, it shows how much money is tied up in stagnant inventory.

Another feature is predictive profitability. The dashboard uses an ARIMA time-series model to forecast hospital demand, then subtracts existing downstream inventory to recommend a more efficient production plan. This connects forecasting with inventory control and profit planning, which was the main analytical purpose of the project.

Building this Control Tower helped me think more carefully about how data products should combine storytelling, technical modeling, and decision support. A dashboard should not only display information. It should help users understand what matters, why it matters, and what choices become possible once the information is organized clearly.

I plan to keep refining this work by improving the interface, adding deeper drill-downs, and enhancing the decision-support logic. The project remains a prototype, but it is a useful example of how visualization can make operational and financial problems easier to reason about.
