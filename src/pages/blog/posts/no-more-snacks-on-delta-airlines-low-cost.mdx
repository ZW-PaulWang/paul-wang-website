Delta recently made a small change that says something much bigger about the airline industry.

Starting May 19, 2026, Delta will stop offering complimentary snacks and drinks on many short flights under about 350 miles for Main Cabin and Delta Comfort passengers. First Class passengers will still receive service, and longer flights will still have snacks and drinks. So this is not a total service cut. But it is still symbolically important, because Delta has long positioned itself as a more premium airline. When a premium airline starts removing small benefits from economy passengers, people naturally ask: are large U.S. airlines becoming more like low cost carriers? ([Southern Living](https://www.southernliving.com/delta-change-on-shorter-flights-11967114))

This is not the only example. American Airlines announced that Basic Economy tickets bought on or after December 17, 2025 will no longer earn AAdvantage miles or Loyalty Points. That is a direct cut to the value of the cheapest ticket. Southwest, which used to be famous for customer friendly policies, is also changing. It has moved toward assigned seating and now charges checked bag fees for many fare types. At the same time, Spirit Airlines, one of the most famous ultra low cost carriers in the U.S., has ceased operations after financial distress and bankruptcy. ([American Airlines](https://www.aa.com/web/i18n/travel-info/experience/seats/basic-economy.html))

At first glance, all of this seems to suggest one simple story: large airlines are cutting passenger benefits, and the whole industry is moving toward the low cost model.

But I think that story is too simple.

Large airlines are not trying to become low cost airlines. They are trying to become more segmented airlines.

The key difference is this: a low cost airline tries to make almost everything cheap and bare bones. A segmented airline offers different levels of service to different customers. The cheapest passenger gets less. The flexible business traveler, frequent flyer, credit card holder, and premium cabin passenger still get more.

This is why American can cut mileage earning from Basic Economy while also adding free WiFi for AAdvantage members. On the surface, one policy cuts benefits and the other adds benefits. But both policies serve the same purpose: push customers into the airline's loyalty ecosystem, and make the cheapest fare less attractive unless the passenger only cares about price.

This is classic price discrimination.

Airlines face very different types of customers. Some passengers just want the lowest price. Some want a free checked bag. Some want seat choice. Some want flexibility. Some care about lounge access, miles, upgrades, and status. If the airline gives everyone the same product, it leaves money on the table. So airlines break the ticket into pieces: base fare, seat selection, checked bag, carry on, WiFi, boarding priority, flexibility, miles, and premium upgrades.

This allows the airline to charge different effective prices to different customers without openly saying, "I know you are willing to pay more."

The basic fare becomes an entry point. It appears in search results. It competes with low cost carriers. But after that, the airline can earn more through add ons, bundles, loyalty programs, and credit cards.

So why did large airlines not make these moves earlier?

Partly because they did not want to damage their brands. Delta, United, American, and Southwest all had different versions of a value proposition. Delta wanted to be more premium. Southwest wanted to be simple and friendly. American and United wanted to balance large networks with loyalty benefits. If they cut too much too early, customers might leave.

But the market environment has changed.

First, costs have risen. Fuel, labor, aircraft maintenance, airport operations, and financing costs all matter. Jet fuel is especially important because airlines cannot fully control it. Recent reports show that fuel cost pressure has been a major issue for U.S. airlines, and Spirit's collapse was worsened by a sharp fuel cost increase. ([Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/legal/litigation/spirit-airlines-says-it-has-no-choice-liquidate-operations-2026-05-04/))

Second, customers have become used to unbundled pricing. In the past, charging separately for bags or seat choice felt strange. Now it is normal. Once passengers become used to paying separately, large airlines can move more benefits out of the base fare without creating as much shock.

Third, large airlines have better data and stronger loyalty systems. They know more about customer behavior. They can estimate who is price sensitive, who is likely to pay for extras, who is valuable through credit card spending, and who is likely to return. That makes price discrimination more precise.

Fourth, competition from low cost airlines has weakened. When Spirit was still a strong national competitor, large airlines had to be careful. If they cut too much, price sensitive passengers could leave. But if ultra low cost carriers are financially weaker, large airlines have more room to reduce benefits on the cheapest fares.

This leads to the next question: why does this make business harder for low cost airlines?

The answer is that large airlines can now imitate the low cost fare without becoming low cost airlines.

A large network airline can offer a very cheap Basic Economy ticket. That ticket may come with fewer benefits, no mileage earning, less flexibility, and more restrictions. To the passenger searching online, it may look close to a low cost carrier's fare. But behind that cheap fare, the large airline still has advantages: a bigger route network, more frequent flights, business travelers, premium cabins, airport slots, credit card revenue, and loyalty programs.

A low cost airline does not have all of these cushions. Its business model depends more heavily on filling planes with price sensitive passengers and collecting fees. When fuel rises or demand weakens, it has less room to absorb the shock. If large airlines can compete on headline price while still earning premium and loyalty revenue elsewhere, the low cost airline gets squeezed from both sides.

This is the core paradox: large airlines are not becoming low cost airlines, but they are making the low cost airline model harder to defend.

They can say to price sensitive customers: "We also have a cheap fare."

And they can say to higher value customers: "We also have better seats, better status, better lounges, and better networks."

That is a much stronger position.

So what is the future of the airline industry?

I expect the market to become more divided, not more equal.

At the bottom, Basic Economy will become more limited. Passengers who choose the cheapest fare should expect fewer included benefits, fewer miles, less flexibility, and more restrictions.

In the middle, airlines will push bundles. Instead of a simple ticket, passengers will be asked to choose packages: seat plus bag, seat plus flexibility, WiFi plus miles, or extra legroom plus priority boarding.

At the top, premium travel will remain important. Airlines will keep investing in lounges, premium cabins, loyalty status, and credit card partnerships because these customers are more profitable.

For investors, the key is not whether an airline gives free snacks. The key is whether the airline has pricing power. Airlines with strong networks, strong loyalty programs, premium demand, and good cost control are better positioned. Airlines that only compete on low price face much higher risk, especially when fuel prices rise or demand softens.

For passengers, the best response is simple: stop comparing only the ticket price.

Compare the full cost of the trip. Does the fare include a checked bag? Does it include a carry on? Can I choose my seat? Do I earn miles? Can I change the ticket? Is WiFi included? Will I need to buy food at the airport? A $120 ticket can become more expensive than a $160 ticket once all the missing pieces are added back.

The Delta snack change is small. But it points to a bigger shift.

Airlines are not just transporting passengers anymore. They are designing menus of choices. The cheapest version of air travel will keep getting more basic. The better version will still exist, but passengers will increasingly have to pay for it, earn it through loyalty, or access it through status and credit cards.

So the destination of the airline industry is not exactly "low cost."

It is something more precise: low cost at the bottom, premium at the top, and a growing price wall between the two.
