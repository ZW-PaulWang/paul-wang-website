I recently started exploring a simplified trading framework I call Simple Nine, inspired by the classic TD Sequential idea. This is not meant to be a full implementation of TD Sequential. Instead, it is my attempt to preserve the basic intuition of the setup while making the rules easier to test, execute, and explain.

The original TD framework is rich, but it can become complicated in practice once qualifiers, cancellation rules, and multiple timeframes are added. For early experimentation, I wanted a structure with clear entry conditions, clear invalidation logic, and rules that could be backtested quickly before becoming more elaborate.

At a high level, Simple Nine tries to identify short-term exhaustion. In a down move, repeated weak closes can suggest that sellers may be losing strength and that a rebound is becoming more likely. In an up move, repeated strong closes can suggest buyer exhaustion and the possibility of a pullback. The number nine is a compact way to describe repeated pressure before looking for reversal confirmation.

For a long setup, the framework tracks bars where the close is weaker than a prior reference close. When enough qualifying bars appear in sequence, the setup marks a potential rebound zone. I do not treat the count itself as an entry signal. I still want confirmation, such as a higher close or a break above recent minor resistance.

For a short setup, the logic works in the opposite direction. The framework tracks bars where the close is stronger than a prior reference close. When the count reaches the setup threshold, it marks a possible pullback zone. Again, I would only enter after confirmation, such as a lower close or a break below recent minor support.

Risk management is part of the strategy rather than an afterthought. A stop-loss should sit beyond the setup extreme, such as the recent local low for a long trade or the recent local high for a short trade. Position size should be based on fixed risk per trade rather than fixed share or contract size. I also prefer predefined profit-taking rules and a no-chase rule, where a trade is skipped if confirmation happens too far away from the original setup zone.

The next step is testing. I want to compare confirmation triggers, examine whether trend filters improve results, study how setup quality changes across assets and timeframes, and measure sensitivity to transaction costs and slippage.

This post is a working note, not a final claim. I will continue updating the strategy as I run more structured backtests and compare alternative rule variants. This is educational content only and not investment advice.
