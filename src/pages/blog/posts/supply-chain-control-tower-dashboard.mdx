This post summarizes my project on designing and developing an interactive Supply Chain Control Tower dashboard built with R and Shiny. The goal was to show how data visualization can transform fragmented operational records into a clearer end-to-end view of supply chain performance.

In many real-world operations, managers can see only one part of the supply chain at a time. That limited visibility makes it harder to identify where delays, bottlenecks, and financial inefficiencies begin. Instead of treating production, shipping, inventory, and sales as isolated steps, the dashboard brings them into one connected analytical system.

The project focuses on a simulated medical device supply chain with four sequential nodes: manufacturer, supplier, seller, and hospital. The central idea is to demonstrate the bullwhip effect, where small changes in final demand at the hospital level can generate much larger fluctuations upstream in production and inventory.

The project uses synthetic but statistically realistic data rather than proprietary business data. That choice makes the system easier to design, explain, and evaluate while preserving realistic operational logic. Each device-level record is linked by a unique device ID and includes timestamps for manufacturing, shipment, warehouse arrival, sale, and hospital deployment.

The dataset also includes financial measures at each stage of the chain. These include manufacturer production cost and wholesale price, supplier markup to the seller, and seller final price to the hospital. This structure allows the dashboard to track both the physical movement of goods and the economic flow of margins.

The dashboard is organized around three managerial questions. The first view gives a high-level snapshot of system health, including devices in transit, stagnant inventory, average end-to-end lead time, and current-quarter gross profit. This view is designed to answer a basic question quickly: is the supply chain operating efficiently right now?

The second view focuses on flow and bottlenecks. It uses a Sankey diagram to visualize movement from manufacturer to supplier, seller, and hospital. The flow widths represent the volume of inventory moving through the system, so accumulation at one node becomes visually obvious.

I also designed this view to support two modes. Unit volume mode focuses on physical inventory flow, while capital mode focuses on financial exposure tied up in inventory. This makes the dashboard useful not only for operations analysis but also for understanding how much capital is trapped in slow-moving stock.

The third view extends the dashboard beyond descriptive analytics. Using hospital consumption as the proxy for final demand, the project applies an ARIMA forecasting model to estimate next-quarter demand. That forecast is then adjusted for downstream inventory already held by suppliers and sellers. In simple terms, required production equals forecasted hospital demand minus existing downstream inventory.

What interests me most about this project is the combination of simulation design, operational storytelling, and financial reasoning. The dashboard is not meant to be a production enterprise tool, but it serves as a conceptual prototype for how integrated dashboards can improve supply chain decisions.

The project also highlights a broader point: better visualization does not simply make data look nicer. It can make system-wide inefficiencies easier to detect, easier to explain, and easier to act on.

This project helped me think more carefully about how data products should bridge technical modeling and practical business decisions. I plan to continue refining the work by improving interaction design, extending the forecasting logic, and exploring more realistic scenario analysis.
